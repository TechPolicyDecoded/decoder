## What this bill does

The AI Accountability Act would require any company deploying an AI system that makes consequential decisions — in hiring, credit, housing, healthcare, or criminal justice — to submit that system to an independent third-party audit every two years.

Audit results would be filed with the FTC and made publicly available in summary form.

## Key provisions

- **Covered systems**: AI used in high-stakes decisions affecting individuals (employment, credit, housing, healthcare, criminal justice)
- **Audit requirement**: Independent third-party audits every 24 months, assessing accuracy, bias, and explainability
- **Disclosure**: Audit summaries published to a public FTC registry
- **Enforcement**: Civil penalties up to $10M per violation; FTC rulemaking authority to define "high-risk"

## What supporters say

Proponents argue this closes a significant accountability gap. Today, companies can deploy AI systems with no external check on whether they discriminate, make systematic errors, or produce decisions users can understand or challenge. The bill borrows from the EU AI Act's risk-based framework but is narrower and enforcement-focused.

## What critics say

Industry groups and some academics argue the bill's audit requirements are technically underdefined — it's unclear what "passing" an audit looks like, and third-party auditors lack standardized methodologies. Small companies may bear disproportionate compliance costs compared to large incumbents, who can absorb audits more easily. Some civil liberties groups want stronger requirements, including public access to model documentation.

## Current status

Introduced in the Senate Commerce Committee in March 2025. A markup session was scheduled for June 2025 but postponed. No House companion bill has been introduced.
